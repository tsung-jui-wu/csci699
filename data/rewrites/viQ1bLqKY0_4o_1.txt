The rapid advancement of language models necessitates benchmarks that accurately reflect their capabilities and generalize to real-world applications. We present EXecution-Eval (EXE), a novel benchmark designed to evaluate large language models (LLMs) on code execution and program state prediction tasks. EXE addresses critical gaps in existing benchmarks, such as limited scalability, lack of task diversity, susceptibility to training data contamination, and cost inefficiencies. Our dataset comprises over 30,000 tasks sourced from 1,000 widely-used Python repositories on GitHub, encompassing varied program lengths and complexities. These tasks challenge models to perform operations like mathematical reasoning, logical inference, and loop execution, while maintaining multiple internal states. Our systematic methodology involves preprocessing repositories, generating diverse input sets, and deriving ground truth outputs, enabling continuous task generation with minimal token usage, thus mitigating test set contamination. Evaluations of state-of-the-art LLMs on EXE reveal significant struggles with complex multi-step tasks, pinpointing specific computational difficulties. Additionally, EXE demonstrates potential in error prediction, offering insights into models’ cybersecurity capabilities. By providing a dynamic and challenging testbed, EXE aims to drive the assessment and improvement of frontier LLMs, illuminating their mechanistic advancements.
